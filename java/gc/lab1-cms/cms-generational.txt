1. The Two-Part System
In a generational JVM, the heap is split into different "rooms" based on the age of the objects. CMS only manages one of those rooms.

Young Generation (Managed by ParNew): This is where new objects are born. Most objects die here quickly. When this space fills up, a "Minor GC" occurs.

Old Generation (Managed by CMS): Objects that survive several rounds of Minor GCs are promoted ("tenured") to this space. This is where CMS does its "Concurrent Mark Sweep" work.



3. How the Generations "Talk"
Even though they are separate, they must coordinate. As we discussed earlier, they use a Card Table to track references between the generations.

If a long-lived object in the CMS (Old Gen) refers to a new object in the ParNew (Young Gen), the JVM marks a "card" so that the Young Gen collector knows not to delete that new object.




4. The Weakness of the Generational Split in CMS
While being generational makes CMS fast, it creates the specific problem you saw in your logs: Promotion Failure.

Because the Young and Old generations are strictly separated:

ParNew cleans the 8 GB Young Gen and tries to push "survivors" into the Old Gen.

CMS manages the 56 GB Old Gen but does not compact it.

If the Old Gen is too fragmented (full of small holes), it can't accept the objects ParNew is trying to send over, even if there is "technically" enough total space.

Summary
Yes, CMS is generational. It relies on the Young Generation (ParNew) to filter out the "infant mortality" of objects so that it only has to manage the long-term residents of the heap.

This is exactly why we use the flag -XX:+CMSScavengeBeforeRemark—it forces a "Young" collection to happen before the "Old" collection finishes, ensuring the two generations are perfectly in sync.
